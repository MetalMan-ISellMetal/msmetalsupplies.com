Galvanizing vs Electro-Galvanizing

An overview of two major zinc-coating technologies used in modern steel production.

Hot-Dip Galvanizing (GI) – Process Overview

Hot-dip galvanizing is a long-established method of protecting steel from corrosion by immersing it into a bath of molten zinc at around 450°C. Before entering the zinc bath, the steel is thoroughly cleaned to remove oils, rust, scale, and impurities. During immersion, zinc reacts with the steel surface to form multiple zinc-iron alloy layers, topped with a final layer of pure metallic zinc. These metallurgical layers are tightly bonded, giving GI steel its signature durability and long service life.

Benefits of GI Coating

GI steel offers excellent corrosion protection through a combination of barrier protection and sacrificial (cathodic) protection. Even if the coating is scratched, the zinc will corrode in place of the exposed steel, slowing rust formation. This makes GI suitable for outdoor applications, structural work, roofing, fencing, infrastructure, safety barriers, and industrial hardware. A properly applied GI coating can last decades depending on thickness and environment.

Electro-Galvanizing (EG) – Process Overview

Electro-galvanizing deposits a thin, extremely uniform layer of pure zinc onto cold-rolled steel using an electrolytic cell. The steel coil passes through a series of zinc ion baths while an electric current causes zinc particles to bond to the metal surface. This allows coating thickness to be controlled with micrometer precision, typically ranging from 3–12 µm. After plating, the material is rinsed, dried, and treated with anti-fingerprint or chromate coatings to improve corrosion resistance.

Benefits of EG Coating

EG steel is prized for its exceptionally smooth and bright surface finish. Because the coating is pure zinc without alloy layers, it remains highly ductile and ideal for forming, stamping, or deep-drawing. The surface also bonds extremely well with paint and welds cleanly. These qualities make EG a standard material for automotive panels, electrical enclosures, appliances, cabinets, and precision components.

GI vs EG – Key Differences

Although both coatings use zinc for corrosion protection, their properties differ significantly due to their manufacturing processes.

Aspect Hot-Dip Galvanizing (GI) Electro-Galvanizing (EG)
Coating Thickness Thicker (40–120+ µm) Thin & controlled (3–12 µm)
Surface Finish Spangled / rougher texture Smooth, bright, uniform
Corrosion Resistance Excellent for outdoor use Moderate; best indoors or painted
Formability Lower flexibility due to alloy layers Very high; ideal for stamping & deep draw
Cost Lower to moderate Higher (cold-rolled base + plating)
Typical Use Roofing, fences, outdoor structures Automotive, electronics, appliances

Which One Should You Choose?

If long-term outdoor corrosion protection is the priority, GI is the stronger choice due to its thicker coating and sacrificial behavior. If surface quality, formability, or paint adhesion is crucial, EG is clearly superior. Many manufacturers use both types depending on the product’s function and the environmental conditions.
